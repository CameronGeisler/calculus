Proof using increments.

With Leibniz notation, let \(\Delta u = g(x + h) - g(x), \Delta y = f(x + h) - f(x)\text{.}\) Then,

\begin{align*} \frac{dy}{dx} \amp = \lim_{\Delta x \to 0} \frac{\Delta y}{\Delta x}\\ \amp = \lim_{\Delta x \to 0} \frac{\Delta y}{\Delta u} \cdot \frac{\Delta u}{\Delta x} \amp\amp \text{multiplying and dividing by $\Delta u$, and rearranging}\\ \amp = \lim_{\Delta x \to 0} \frac{\Delta y}{\Delta u} \cdot \lim_{\Delta x \to 0} \frac{\Delta u}{\Delta x} \amp\amp \text{by the limit product rule} \end{align*}

Then, the limit on the right is \(\frac{du}{dx} = g'(x)\text{.}\) Also, since \(y = f(u)\) is differentiable at \(u = g(x)\text{,}\) it is continuous, and so as \(\Delta x \to 0\text{,}\) \(\Delta u \to 0\) also. Thus,

\begin{align*} \frac{dy}{dx} \amp = \lim_{\Delta u \to 0} \frac{\Delta y}{\Delta u} \cdot \lim_{\Delta x \to 0} \frac{\Delta u}{\Delta x}\\ \amp = \frac{dy}{du} \cdot \frac{du}{dx} \end{align*}
